What is the Problem? Why is it important?


Under suboptimal conditions the availability of mobile service may reduce. The Philippines, Vanuatu, and Tonga, are examples of countries which are disproportionately affected by natural disasters such as hurricanes and earthquakes. Specific carrier drop-out can leave certain end-users without service, even when they are in close proximity to unaffected devices on other providers. Affected users may be unable to contact emergency services or communicate critical information during outages. Perhaps even more importantly, the affected users may be the emergency services themselves.


What has been done? Why are they not sufficient?


     1. ROGER: Robust and rapidly deployable GSM base station and Backhaul for Emergency Response.

           - high cost of deployment

           - centralized,  risk of the base station being damaged


     2. E-Explorer: Emergency Communication and Quick Seismic Damage Investigation Based on Smartphone.

           - Wifi/Bluetooth link gives only short range benefit


     3. HELPER: Heterogeneous Efficient Low Power Radio for Enabling Ad Hoc Emergency Public Safety Networks.

           - Uses vehicles, drones, and buildings with transmitters and connects them in a mesh topology

           - routing algorithm aims to maximize network lifetime


     4. LOCATE: LoRa-based mObile emergenCy mAnagement sysTEm.

           - phone based distributed network using flood based routing to find nearby emergency responders


LoRa-based Mesh Network for Off-grid Emergency Communications[1] is existing research which the above information is sourced from. It seeks to combine the benefits of examples 3 and 4 by using existing (out of service) mobile devices to create data, transmitting that data via a bluetooth link to a radio transponder, and then floods the LoRa signal to a wide local area.

This is a decent solution for emergency situations. Of course, it requires users to have, at the ready, these transponders. To steel-man the idea, the transponders would be significantly cheaper than the average satellite phone, and similarly to emergency help buttons already implemented in many cities, governments could provide these as local infrastructure.

Situations such as these illustrate a critical demand for robust, long range, and available ad hoc networking. Additionally, there is even more demand with lower imperative, outside the scope of this project.


What's your approach? Why can it do better or differently?


Mobile wireless ad hoc mesh networks[2] developed under military contexts[3] are becoming increasingly viable solutions for the public and private sector and have clear and already implemented applications in emergency response. They are also likely to be useful for fog computing, and the endless networks of other robots soon to come.

Ad hoc mesh networks are vulnerable to partition, since they are (in the purest sense) raised solely by the sparsely connected mobile clients using the network. Autonomous vehicles and robots can act as fully-mobile local-area access points, and can organize topologically to meet the specific needs of the ad hoc network they are assisting. These robot super nodes can overlay the ad hoc network making it more robust and aid in the creation of foggy links (via daisy chain topology or access capabilities outside of the ad hoc scope) to wider networks such as the internet.


Specifically I will conduct analysis on packet-loss and throughput between nodes in simulation of a sparsely connected mobile ad hoc mesh network with and without assistance of a mobile overlay.
